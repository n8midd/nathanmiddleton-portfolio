Mentoring in quality engineering is not handing someone your old test scripts and hoping they figure it out.

It is deliberately transferring judgment: how to prioritize, how to debug, how to push back on bad requirements, and how to write automation that survives the next refactor.

The teams I have seen grow fastest treat mentoring as part of the job—not a volunteer activity squeezed in when deadlines allow.

---

## Mentoring Is Not Shadowing Forever

Shadowing has a shelf life. After a few weeks, mentees need reps with real accountability.

I structure early mentorship in phases:

**Observe and narrate:** Mentor explains decisions aloud during triage, PR reviews, and planning.

**Co-own:** Mentee leads a task; mentor stays available but does not take the keyboard back at the first error.

**Own with review:** Mentee delivers a feature area—fixtures, page objects, CI job—with scheduled review checkpoints.

**Teach back:** Mentee documents a pattern or runs a lunch-and-learn. Teaching solidifies learning.

If someone is still shadowing after six months, the mentorship design is broken—not the person.

---

## What to Teach First

Junior SDETs often arrive with strong tool syntax but weak failure analysis. I prioritize:

1. Reading traces, logs, and network tabs before changing locators
2. Mapping tests to user journeys and business risk
3. Writing stable selectors and waiting strategies
4. Opening PRs that explain *what* broke and *why* the fix works

Framework trivia can wait. Judgment under uncertainty cannot.

---

## Feedback That Actually Helps

Generic feedback ("make this cleaner") wastes cycles. I use:

- **Intent questions:** "What behavior are you proving?"
- **Future maintainer test:** "If this UI changes tomorrow, what breaks first?"
- **Scope checks:** "Could a unit or API test cover this faster?"

Praise specific decisions, not effort alone. "Good call waiting for network idle here—here is why that mattered" beats "nice work."

---

## Mentoring Across Levels

Mid-level engineers need different support: influencing without authority, estimating automation ROI, and navigating conflict with product timelines.

Senior engineers benefit from mentorship too—executive communication, hiring participation, and org-level strategy. Leadership mentoring is often neglected because seniors are assumed to "already know."

Create peer mentorship pairs across teams. Some of the best growth I have seen came from SDETs in different product lines reviewing each other's frameworks.

---

## Where This Goes Wrong

**Hero mentors:** One senior carries all teaching load and becomes a bottleneck.

**Sink or swim:** New hires thrown into a 4,000-test repo with no map. They learn bad habits or burn out.

**No time protected:** Mentorship scheduled "when we have bandwidth"—which never comes during crunch.

**Copy-paste culture:** Mentees mimic mentor's code without understanding principles. Everything looks the same and breaks the same way.

**Gatekeeping:** Mentors who hoard interesting work and assign only maintenance. Growth stalls; retention suffers.

---

## Measuring Mentorship

Output metrics alone mislead. I look for:

- Decreasing time-to-diagnosis for mentee-owned failures
- Increasing quality of PR descriptions and test design docs
- Mentees initiating improvements without being asked
- Willingness to challenge bad ideas respectfully in meetings

Mentoring succeeds when your mentee no longer needs you in the room—and still invites you because collaboration is valuable.

That is the bar worth building toward.
