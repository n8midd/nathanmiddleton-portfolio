Metrics shape behavior. If you measure test count, you get test count—not quality.

Quality leaders choose metrics that predict customer pain, guide investment, and survive scrutiny from engineers who know when numbers lie.

The goal is decision support, not dashboard decoration.

---

## Metrics Worth Tracking

**Escaped defects** — bugs found in production by severity and area. The ultimate outcome metric if classified honestly.

**Flake rate** — percentage of failures that pass on retry without code changes. Leading indicator of trust erosion.

**Pipeline feedback time** — how long developers wait for actionable signal on PRs.

**MTTR for test failures** — mean time to diagnose and fix broken automation or confirmed app bugs.

**Deployment frequency and change failure rate** — DORA metrics contextualize quality work in delivery performance.

**Test maintenance ratio** — time spent fixing tests vs. writing new ones. Rising ratio means architectural or process debt.

---

## Metrics to Treat Carefully

**Automation coverage %** — only meaningful with explicit scope (lines, journeys, APIs). Easy to game and demoralizing when misused.

**Total test count** — activity metric, not outcome.

**Pass rate alone** — green builds with ignored tests or weak assertions lie.

**Defects found in QA** — incentivizes finding bugs late instead of preventing them early.

Use these with context or not at all.

---

## Making Metrics Actionable

Every metric on a dashboard needs:

- **Owner** who explains week-over-week movement
- **Threshold** that triggers action (not panic)
- **Linked ritual** — weekly ops review, flake burndown, incident retro

Metrics without rituals become wallpaper.

---

## Segment, Do Not Aggregate Blindly

Org-wide pass rate hides a burning payments suite. Aggregate escaped defects mask one team's regression.

Segment by squad, service, or customer journey. Fix the hottest segment first.

Executives need rollups; working teams need drill-down.

---

## Qualitative Signals Matter

Surveys and conversation catch what metrics miss:

- Do developers merge with confidence?
- Do teams rerun CI "until green" as standard practice?
- Do product managers trust release notes?

If qualitative trust is low while metrics look fine, your metrics are wrong or incomplete.

---

## Where This Goes Wrong

**Metric mandates from afar:** Leadership sets coverage targets without capacity or strategy.

**Gaming:** Quarantining tests, weakening assertions, splitting suites to hide duration.

**Too many KPIs:** Everything is tracked; nothing is owned.

**No baseline:** Teams cannot show improvement because history was never recorded.

**Blame metrics:** Used in performance reviews to punish instead of improve systems.

---

## The Metric Test

Before adding a metric, ask:

> **If this number improves, will customers or developers measurably benefit?**

If the honest answer is unclear, do not put it on the executive slide.

Quality metrics should simplify decisions—not create a second job of explaining why the numbers are misleading.
