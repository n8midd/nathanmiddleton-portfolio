Mentoring builds capability over months. Coaching adjusts behavior this week.

In quality engineering, coaching shows up everywhere: a standup where someone blames "flaky automation," a PR review that adds ten more sleeps, a retro where the team vows to "add more tests" without addressing root cause.

Coaching is the art of helping someone see a better move in real time—without taking over their job.

---

## Coaching vs. Managing vs. Mentoring

**Managing** sets priorities and removes blockers. **Mentoring** develops long-term skill. **Coaching** sharpens specific behaviors in specific contexts.

You can manage without coaching (tasks get done, growth stalls). You can coach without formal authority (peers coach peers effectively).

I coach when I notice a repeatable pattern: skipping reproduction steps, over-automating, avoiding difficult conversations with dev leads, or treating green builds as proof of quality.

---

## The Question-First Habit

The fastest way to shut down learning is jumping in with the answer.

When an engineer says, "The test is flaky," I might ask:

- What changed since it last passed reliably?
- Is it failing in one environment or all?
- Does it fail on retry?
- What would convince a developer this is an app bug?

Questions build diagnostic muscle. Answers provide one fix for one day.

---

## Coaching Moments That Matter

**In triage:** Walk through trace reading once, then let them lead the next failure while you ask guiding questions.

**In design reviews:** Challenge scope—"What risk does this test reduce?"—before discussing implementation.

**In incidents:** Coach communication: facts, customer impact, next steps—not blame language.

**In 1:1s:** Pick one behavior theme (e.g., proactive status updates) for two weeks instead of ten vague improvement goals.

---

## Feedback Timing and Tone

Public correction humiliates; public recognition motivates. Hard conversations about quality gaps belong in private 1:1s with specific examples.

Coaching feedback should be:

- **Timely** — close to the event
- **Specific** — reference the test, PR, or meeting
- **Actionable** — one next behavior to try
- **Supportive** — assume good intent

---

## Coaching Under Pressure

During release crunches, coaching often disappears. That is when bad habits cement: disabling tests, merging with red builds, skipping root cause.

I protect small coaching rituals even in crunch: five-minute post-mortems on failed builds, paired triage on the worst flake, a daily "one thing we learned" Slack note.

Short cycles beat quarterly training when stress is high.

---

## Where This Goes Wrong

**Fixing for them:** Senior takes keyboard every time. Coachee never builds confidence.

**Vague pep talks:** "Be more proactive" without defining what proactive looks like in your org.

**Coaching as punishment:** Feedback only when something breaks. People hide problems.

**One-size-fits-all:** Introverts and extroverts, juniors and mids, need different coaching styles.

**Confusing authority:** Coaching requires trust. If the coachee thinks every session ends in a performance rating, honesty stops.

---

## The Behavior Change Test

Good coaching shows up in changed habits within weeks—not perfect tests, but better instincts.

Ask: **What did this person do differently last sprint that they were not doing before?**

If the answer is nothing, coaching was conversation—not development.

That is the difference between a leader who talks about quality and one who grows it person by person.
